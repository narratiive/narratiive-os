# Narratiive Signal — Editorial Style Guide

Version: 1.1
Status: Canonical

## Voice

Write like an intelligent founder speaking to another founder.

The voice is:

- warm, observant and direct;
- editorial rather than consultancy-led;
- curious rather than falsely certain;
- commercially literate without sounding financialised;
- imaginative without becoming theatrical;
- concise without becoming abrupt;
- human enough to use first person occasionally when it improves naturalness.

The closest posture is a sharp strategic dispatch or an experienced strategy director's field note, not a newsletter, report or presentation transcript.

## The core editorial test

Every Signal should articulate something the recipient may already sense but has not yet heard expressed clearly.

The document should create curiosity before it creates completeness.

## Preferred language

Prefer:

- `What we noticed`
- `Why we think that`
- `The white space`
- `If this were ours...`
- `What we'd be careful about`
- `If this sparks a conversation...`

Use plain English and concrete nouns. Name the business tension directly.

## Avoid

Do not use:

- `unlock`, `leverage`, `synergy`, `full-funnel`, `best-in-class`, `game-changing`;
- `our proprietary framework`;
- inflated declarations about transformation;
- generic compliments such as `we love what you're building` unless followed by a precise reason;
- agency theatre, management-consultancy language or academic padding;
- excessive em dashes, rhetorical questions or fragments;
- invented consumer quotes or unsupported statistics;
- long scene-setting passages before the central observation;
- repeated summaries of the same insight in different language;
- exhaustive recommendation lists;
- language explaining how much research, workflow or AI effort went into the output.

## Sentence and paragraph rules

- Keep paragraphs short.
- Vary sentence length naturally.
- Use one central idea throughout.
- Prefer specific observations to adjectives.
- Remove any sentence that merely restates the heading.
- Do not begin every section with the company name.
- Use no more than three evidence bullets in the recipient-facing Signal.
- Each section should advance the argument rather than prove thoroughness.
- When choosing between an additional insight and more space, choose more space.

## Evidence posture

Evidence should make the observation credible, not make the writer look clever.

- Distinguish fact, interpretation and assumption.
- Cite public evidence internally and retain source links in the work record.
- Do not overstate what social comments, reviews or small samples prove.
- Never manufacture precision.
- Select only the evidence required for the recipient to trust the argument.
- Put detailed evidence lineage and source notes in the internal work record, not the final Signal.

## Outreach email style

The outreach email should normally be 100–150 words and must remain below 180 words.

It should fit in one standard laptop viewport without scrolling.

Recommended flow:

1. Explain why Narratiive spent time looking at this particular business.
2. Share the central observation in one or two sentences.
3. Explain that a short Signal and, where approved, speculative creative are attached or linked.
4. Close with a soft invitation.

The email should not summarise the entire Signal, reproduce its evidence, include a hard calendar link or explain the internal process.

## Recipient-facing Signal style

- Write for a three-minute read.
- Lead with the strongest observation quickly.
- Keep the full piece between 500 and 800 words where possible and below 900 words.
- Make one argument, not a collection of clever thoughts.
- The strategic direction should be vivid enough to picture but incomplete enough to invite discussion.
- Use one dominant visual or up to two concise creative frames.
- Do not expose alternative routes, rejected ideas, prompts, scores, research notes or operating instructions.

## Creative language

Describe creative with enough sensory specificity to guide production, but do not write an overlong film treatment. State:

- the emotional job;
- the central image or behaviour;
- the setting and visual texture;
- the role of the product or brand;
- the intended audience response.

Taste is more important than spectacle.

In the recipient-facing Signal, compress the creative world into a short, desirable articulation. Detailed scene construction, sound design, exclusions and Higgsfield-ready prompts belong in the separate internal production brief.

## Final read-aloud test

Before approval, read the piece aloud. It should sound like a thoughtful person, not an AI, a pitch deck or a templated sales sequence.

Then apply the subtraction test: remove anything that proves effort but does not increase clarity, credibility, desire or curiosity.
