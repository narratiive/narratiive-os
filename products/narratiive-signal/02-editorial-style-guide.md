# Narratiive Signal — Editorial Style Guide

Version: 1.0
Status: Canonical

## Voice

Write like an intelligent founder speaking to another founder.

The voice is:

- warm, observant and direct;
- editorial rather than consultancy-led;
- curious rather than falsely certain;
- commercially literate without sounding financialised;
- imaginative without becoming theatrical;
- concise without becoming abrupt.

## The core editorial test

Every Signal should articulate something the recipient may already sense but has not yet heard expressed clearly.

## Preferred language

Prefer:

- `What we noticed`
- `Why we think that`
- `The white space`
- `If this were ours...`
- `What we'd be careful about`
- `If this sparks a conversation...`

Use plain English and concrete nouns. Name the business tension directly.

## Avoid

Do not use:

- `unlock`, `leverage`, `synergy`, `full-funnel`, `best-in-class`, `game-changing`;
- `our proprietary framework`;
- inflated declarations about transformation;
- generic compliments such as `we love what you're building` unless followed by a precise reason;
- agency theatre, management-consultancy language or academic padding;
- excessive em dashes, rhetorical questions or fragments;
- invented consumer quotes or unsupported statistics.

## Sentence and paragraph rules

- Keep paragraphs short.
- Vary sentence length naturally.
- Use one central idea throughout.
- Prefer specific observations to adjectives.
- Remove any sentence that merely restates the heading.
- Do not begin every section with the company name.

## Evidence posture

Evidence should make the observation credible, not make the writer look clever.

- Distinguish fact, interpretation and assumption.
- Cite public evidence internally and retain source links in the work record.
- Do not overstate what social comments, reviews or small samples prove.
- Never manufacture precision.

## Email style

The outreach email should normally be 100–180 words.

Recommended flow:

1. Explain that Narratiive spent time looking at the business and why.
2. Share the central observation in one or two sentences.
3. Explain that a short Signal and speculative creative are attached.
4. Close with a soft invitation.

The email should not summarise the entire Signal or force a calendar link.

## Creative language

Describe creative with enough sensory specificity to guide production, but do not write an overlong film treatment. State:

- the emotional job;
- the central image or behaviour;
- the setting and visual texture;
- the role of the product or brand;
- the intended audience response.

Taste is more important than spectacle.

## Final read-aloud test

Before approval, read the piece aloud. It should sound like a thoughtful person, not an AI, a pitch deck or a templated sales sequence.
